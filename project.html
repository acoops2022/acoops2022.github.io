Project

Aim

This project aims to evaluate some common economic objectives and promises made by the conservative party in their 2010, 2015 and 2019 manifestos using data. Using python, I read the three manifestos and counted the most frequent words in ascending order. click here to see my google collab. After sifting through article words, I found the following: ‘tax’ appeared 292 times, ‘business’ 188 times, ‘invest’ 182 times, ‘economy’ 142 times and ‘NHS’ 118 times. After, reading the manifestos I have decided to test the commitments on the following categories. Macroeconomic promises on reducing the budget deficit, improving GDP growth and increasing employment. Investment promises to the improve the NHS redistribute around the UK. This project aims to take a neutral political stance and purely evaluate whether the conservative party achieved their pledges on a yes or no basis.

Taxation

A major commitment of the Conservative manifestos was to improve Britain’s international tax competitiveness and not raise VAT and National Insurance tax. The chart above shows the corporation tax cuts the Conservative party introduced and its effect on corporation tax received by HMRC. The graph demonstrates the Conservative party’s policy of trickle-down economics and the Laffre curve (1974) because by lowering corporation tax they encouraged entrepreneurship and increased tax revenue. Therefore, they achieved one aspect of their taxation pledges.

However, UK tax system is complex and raising or lowering one rate impacts the benefits and incentives of others. Furthermore, analysis became too complex to visualise when looking at data for VAT and NI rate changes due to the effective rate depending on many variables. It should be noted that VAT was raised from 17.5% to 20% in Jan 2011 but during COVID-19 was lowered to 5% and then raised to 12.5% for certain sectors. NI rate was also raised and then reversed during 2022.

I initially tried to source the data through Trading Economics API, however it required an expensive subscription for historical data. Therefore, I copied the data into a csv file along with data from gov.uk.

Levelling up Britain

Another common promise among the manifestos was to redistribute wealth from London to the rest of the UK through investment. This chart is a map of the UK regions. The more red the region the large the amount of government investment the region received. If you move the slider from 2010 to 2021 you can see that there has been little variation in investment across the UK, with London receiving the most investment. This demonstrates an unsubstantiated promise.
